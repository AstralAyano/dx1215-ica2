History


Dungeons & Dragons is a fantasy tabletop role-playing game originally designed by Ernest Gary Gygax and David Arneson.

It was published in 1974 by Tactical Studios Rules, company owned by Gygax. Later on published by Wizards of the Coast since 1997.


Originally it was a set of medieval miniature rules written by Jeff Perren. These were expanded by Gary Gygax, that included a fantasy supplement, before the game was published as Chainmail.

When Dave Wesely entered the Army in 1970, his friend, Dave Arneson a fellow Napoleonics wargamer began a medieval variation of Wesely's Braunstein games, where players control individuals instead of armies.


Arneson used Chainmail to resolve the combat mechanics. As play progressed, Arneson added such innovations as character classes, experience points, level advancement, armor class, and others.

As Arneson previously partnered with Gygax on Don't Give Up the Ship!, Arneson introduced Gygas to his Blackmoor game and the two then collaborated on developing "The Fantasy Game", and that game became Dungeons & Dragons, with the final writing and preparation of the text being done by Gygax.

Evolution


In 1989, Advanced Dungeons & Dragons 2nd Edition was published, it excluded some aspects of the original game that had attracted negative publicity. Such as references to demons and devils, sexually suggestive artwork, and playable, evil-aligned character types (assassins and half-orcs) were removed.

The 2nd Edition also moved away from the "sword and sorcery" fantasy fiction to a mixture of medieval history and mythology.


In 1997, Tactical Studios Rules almost went bankrupt and was purchased by Wizards of the Coast.

After three years of development, Dungeons & Dragons 3rd Edition was released in 2000. The 3rd Edition was the largest revision of the D&D rules to date and served as the basis for a multi-genre role-playing system designed around 20-sided dice, called the d20 System.






The 3rd Edition was designed to be less restrictive than previous editions, allowing players more flexibility to create the characters ' they wanted to play. Skills and feats (features) were also introduced into the core rules to allow further customisation of characters.

In 2003, Dungeons & Dragons v.3.5 was released as a revision of the 3rd Edition rules. This includes hundreds of rule changes and expanded the core rulebooks.


Middle of a D&D game in 2003.



In early 2005, Wizards of the Coast's Research & Development team started the development of Dungeons & Dragons 4th Edition, mostly based on the feedback obtained from the D&D playing community, with a desire to make the game faster, more intuitive, and with a better play experience than under the 3rd Edition.

After going through numerous design phases since May 2005, Dungeons & Dragons 4th Edition was announced at Gen Con in August 2007. 4th Edition introduced numerous rules changes and most of the characters abilities were restructured into "Powers", whereby it altered the spell-using classes by adding abilities that could be used at will, per encounter, or per day. Likewise, non-magic classes also had parallel sets of options.


On January 9, 2012, Wizards of the Coast announced that it was working on a 5th Edition of the game. And the strategy was to take suggestions from players and let them playtest the rules. Public playtesting began on May 24, 2012, and had already received 75,000 playtesters feedback in a single month. This lead to the decision of announcing that it would take at least two years of development to seriously get it right.

In the second half of 2014, Dungeons & Dragons 5th Edition was released, it also coincided with D&D's 40th anniversary.

Ever since the release of 5th Edition, it has garnered a lot of attention world-wide, even breaking the most number of players in history from 12 million to 15 million in 2017 North America. As time goes on, Dungeons & Dragons have become more accessible to everyone world-wide through the use of internet, using a very popular official Dungeons & Dragons website called DNDBeyond.

Basics


When it comes to playing Dungeons & Dragons, it requires one Dungeon Master (DM) and Player(s). It may seem a bit intimidating but I assure you, it isn't and you don't have to follow every single rules in the rulebooks.

So gather some friends! And get ready to play.


As the Dungeon Master, the first step is having a Session 0. Gather all your party members, sit down, and have a talk about what everyone wants to play, The style of game? Genre? Medieval or futuristic? Note down every player's opinion, find a easy simple campaign to start it off!

Once decided you can proceed with character creation. There are plety of guides on how to create a D&D Character, so don't be shy and search it up! Help your players with anything they don't understand.


As the Player, you don't have to let your DM do all the work, you can also search up some basic informations, such as, what type of class do you want to play? What race? Is your character good? or evil? Write it down, this can help your DM guide you through easier and helps offload some work from the DM!
